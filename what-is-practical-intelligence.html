What is practical intelligence?

We have a fair working model of how the brain does its job: neurons connect into networks that recognize patterns and build new ones. From those patterns we make sense of how the world works, and so we are able to shape it.

Practical intelligence is the shaping part: how well you turn your sense of how the world works into actions that change it the way you want.

It is worth holding a clear idea of what that is. When you set out to act intelligently, and to show it to others, in a pitch, an interview, a hard conversation, you are working from some idea of what intelligence is, spoken or not. This page tries to make that idea a clear one.

1The only lever is action

You can understand a situation perfectly and still change nothing. The world only changes when you act on it: when you put energy in and alter its course.

So in the end, all your understanding has to turn into one choice: what should I do.

2Which action? Steering

At any moment you could do many different things. Practical intelligence is choosing the one that steers the situation toward your goal, and away from the outcomes you do not want.

Like turning a wheel, each action tries to nudge the situation toward a different outcome. But the terrain often resists you, like driving over ice or mud, so you have to keep adapting as the situation changes.

3The two aspects of steering

Good steering is two things happening almost at once. One is foresight: predicting where an action leads, so you can pick the one pointed at your goal. The other is correction: acting, seeing what the world does, and adjusting. They run together, and foresight is what decides whether the ride is smooth or rough.

The better you can predict, the less you have to rely on crashing for navigation.

This points at a practical definition of "intelligence": chance has no foresight, an animal a crude one, a careless person a weak one, a sharp person a good one.

4Where foresight comes from

Foresight is not one step but a short chain. First you observe the situation, accurately and fully enough to have something real to work with. Then you find a pattern in it: usually by matching what you see to situations you have met before, and, when nothing fits, by building a new pattern to make sense of it. Only on top of that pattern can you predict what your action is likely to do.

This is why two people in the same situation can predict so differently. The one who looks carefully and carries a deep stock of patterns sees what is coming. The one who looks carelessly, or forces a familiar pattern onto a situation that does not fit, does not. Most of foresight is built right here, in how well you observe and how good your patterns are.

Matching patterns and building them are what the metacognition page calls schemas and sensemaking. This is where the two pages meet.

5You still have to try things

Foresight is never enough. Reality is too complex to fully predict, so at some point you have to act and find out.

What separates intelligent attempts from flailing trial-and-error is not whether you try, but the frame you are trying within, the working pattern you are using to read the situation. Sharper people and sharper teams try on purpose, inside a model they control:

Filling in a frameYou already have a working picture of the situation, and you try things to test it and fill it in. Each try answers a specific question.
Building a frameYou do not have that picture yet, so you try things to work out what is going on and build one. Slower, but still aimed.
No frame at allYou try things at random to see what sticks. Energy into the system with no steering. Each run looks like progress and is likely not.

The first two are how good problem-solvers use frames, and that machinery is its own page, on metacognition. The third is the trap, and it is the easy one to fall into, because it feels productive: you are busy, the screen keeps changing, something is always happening, so motion gets mistaken for progress.

6The longer the game, the harder to steer

Steering one step is easy. Real-life goals sit many steps away, and that is where it gets hard. Across a long chain, small errors add up, the situation drifts while you work, and the paths branch, so the route you planned stops fitting the world you are now in.

The further off the goal, the harder it is to steer toward, which is among the reasons hard problems are hard. It is also why the most valuable goals are the most demanding: the things worth most are built over many steps and a long time, and far-off goals lean less on any single forecast and more on correcting as you go.

7The point

Practical intelligence is not just raw brainpower. It is how reliably the things you do bend the world toward your goal.
